# Flywheel Affiliate Daily

## The Lead

**Agentic commerce just stopped being theoretical.** In the span of a single week, American Express launched fraud protections specifically for AI-agent purchases, Amazon's Rufus assistant gained the ability to autonomously track prices and complete buys, and Walmart deployed its "Sparky" conversational agent across its digital storefront. CBS News and Axios both ran major features yesterday on the trend, with Axios framing it as a trillion-dollar shift in how shopping decisions get made.

The Capgemini research driving much of the conversation puts hard numbers on adoption: 38% of shoppers already trust AI agents to manage routine purchases, and 55% say they'd delegate reorders within three years. Meanwhile, Adobe's holiday data showed AI-driven traffic surging 693% year-over-year, with revenue per AI-referred visit up 254%. These aren't pilot numbers anymore.

For the affiliate channel, this is a structural question, not a feature request. When a consumer asks an AI agent to "find me running shoes under $120," that agent isn't clicking through traditional affiliate links, reading review content, or following influencer recommendations. It's querying product feeds, comparing structured data, and optimizing on price and availability. The affiliate partners who survive this shift will be the ones whose data is machine-readable, whose commission structures incentivize agent referrals, and whose attribution models can handle non-human traffic.

The smart move isn't to panic — it's to build for both channels simultaneously. Human-driven discovery isn't disappearing; it's being supplemented by agent-driven purchasing. Brands that treat these as two separate funnels, with distinct partner strategies for each, will outperform those waiting to see which one wins.

## What's Moving

- **Instagram relaunches creator affiliate commerce.** Creators can now tag affiliate products directly in Reels — three years after Instagram killed its original program. Meta's Nicola Mendelsohn declared "the era of link-in-bio is finally over" at Shoptalk. *Why it matters:* Instagram is trying to claw back creator trust from TikTok Shop ($33B+ GMV in 2024) and LTK ($6B+ annual sales). Brands should test the new tagging but not abandon diversified affiliate links yet.

- **eBay joins Meta's Facebook affiliate beta.** Following Amazon as a launch partner, eBay is now part of Facebook's native affiliate program that lets creators tag products inside posts and Reels without link-in-bio workarounds. *Why it matters:* Meta is building a closed-loop affiliate ecosystem across Instagram and Facebook — if your products aren't in it, you're invisible on two of the largest social platforms.

- **IAB Australia confirms affiliate is a growth channel.** The 2026 State of the Nation report shows 42% of advertisers increased affiliate spend this year, two-thirds of publishers saw revenue growth, and 90% expect the channel's importance to increase. Travel overtook retail as the top-performing publisher vertical. *Why it matters:* Hard data to bring to budget conversations. Affiliate isn't a legacy channel — it's where investment is accelerating.

- **FTC sharpens brand liability for directed influencer content.** New enforcement guidance holds brands — not just creators — directly liable when brand-supplied talking points produce misleading claims or inadequate disclosures. Separately, legal analysis warns that AI-generated influencers face the same disclosure rules as human ones. *Why it matters:* If you're scripting affiliate content for partners, your legal exposure just expanded. Review your creator briefs now.

- **Shopify publishes 2026 commission benchmarks.** Growth-stage brands are running hybrid structures: 15–25% on new customers, 5–10% on returning. Cookie windows below 14 days are flagged as counterproductive for non-impulse products. *Why it matters:* If your commission structure hasn't been updated in 12 months, you're likely underpaying for acquisition and overpaying for retention.

## Partner Landscape

**Profile: AI Shopping Agents as Affiliate Partners**

The week's biggest stories all point in one direction — AI agents are becoming a new class of affiliate partner, whether the industry is ready or not. Amazon's Rufus, Walmart's Sparky, and Ulta Beauty's Gemini-powered shopping assistant (launched with Google at Cloud Next) represent a partner type that didn't exist two years ago.

Unlike traditional affiliates, AI agents don't respond to banner ads, email newsletters, or influencer endorsements. They respond to structured product data, competitive pricing signals, and API-accessible inventory. The "commission" model is still being figured out — Amex's new protections suggest the payments infrastructure is adapting faster than the affiliate networks themselves.

For vendors evaluating this partner type: start by ensuring your product catalog has clean, structured data feeds. Then ask your affiliate network whether they support machine-to-machine attribution. If the answer is "not yet," that's a signal about where the network's roadmap priorities sit.

## The AI Angle

OpenAI quietly scaled back its Instant Checkout feature this month, but the retreat is tactical, not strategic — Sephora, Carrefour, and Ulta Beauty all launched agentic commerce integrations in the same window. Capgemini draws the parallel to marketplace adoption: retailers who sat out DoorDash and Instacart early lost market share they never recovered. The same pattern is forming for agent-compatible commerce, and affiliate programs that can't serve machine queries will lose the referral before it happens.

## One Thing to Do Today

**Audit your product data feed.** Pull up your primary affiliate network's product catalog and check: Are prices current? Are descriptions structured (not just marketing copy)? Are availability flags accurate? AI shopping agents rank products on data quality before anything else — and a stale feed means you don't exist in their results.
