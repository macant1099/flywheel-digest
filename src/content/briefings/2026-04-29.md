# Flywheel Affiliate Daily

## The Lead

**Rakuten × Impact.com: The Alliance That Reshapes Affiliate Infrastructure**

Yesterday, Rakuten International and Impact.com announced a strategic alliance that effectively merges the operational plumbing of the two oldest names in affiliate marketing. Under the deal, Rakuten exits the platform-technology business entirely: its thousands of merchants and publishers will migrate to Impact.com's tracking, contracting, and payment infrastructure. Rakuten refocuses on what it owns — Rakuten Rewards, its managed-service agency business, and first-party consumer data. Impact, in turn, gains Rakuten's massive publisher base and names Rakuten a "Titanium Partner" inside its marketplace.

This is not a merger. No equity changed hands. But the practical effect is consolidation without acquisition. Impact CEO David Yovanno framed it bluntly: after nearly 30 years, the affiliate channel has "put on the muscle" to compete with Google, Amazon, and Meta for always-on performance budgets. A unified Rakuten-Impact stack gives advertisers one contracting surface, one attribution pipeline, and one payment rail across the largest combined publisher footprint in the industry.

For vendors, the signal is clear: the era of fragmented affiliate networks is closing. If you run programs across multiple platforms, expect pressure to consolidate onto fewer, larger ecosystems. Brands already on Impact see an instant expansion of premium publisher inventory. Brands on Rakuten's legacy tech face a forced migration — and the window to negotiate favorable terms is now, during the transition, not after it.

The deeper takeaway is strategic. When your two biggest platform competitors decide to merge their go-to-market, it compresses the middle. Smaller networks will need to differentiate hard — on vertical expertise, creator relationships, or AI tooling — or risk becoming commodity pipes. Vendors should audit their network mix this quarter and decide where they want to place long-term bets.

## Partner Landscape

**Profile: Comparison Engines in the Age of AI**

Comparison engines — the price-check sites, spec-comparison tools, and "best of" aggregators — were the original affiliate workhorses. They thrived on SEO traffic and structured data. But 2026 is an inflection year: AI chatbots now answer comparison queries directly inside search results, compressing the click path that comparison sites depend on.

The survivors are adapting. The strongest comparison publishers are licensing their structured product feeds directly to AI platforms, turning themselves into data suppliers rather than destination sites. Others are building API-first affiliate integrations so that AI shopping agents can query their catalogs programmatically and carry affiliate attribution through the response. A handful are launching their own conversational interfaces, trying to meet consumers where they are instead of waiting for the search click.

For vendors, this means your product data quality matters more than ever. Comparison engines can only recommend you if your feeds are accurate, complete, and machine-readable. Invest in structured data now — it's the connective tissue between your catalog and every AI-powered discovery surface.

## The AI Angle

CBS News reported this week that major retailers and financial institutions are racing to build agentic commerce infrastructure, even as consumer trust lags behind. After OpenAI pulled back on native Instant Checkout in March 2026, the market pivoted to ChatGPT apps where merchants run their own mini-checkout inside the chat. The affiliate implication: whoever controls the agent's product recommendation controls the attribution. Vendors who integrate early into AI agent ecosystems — with clean product feeds and affiliate-trackable endpoints — will capture share before the standards harden.
