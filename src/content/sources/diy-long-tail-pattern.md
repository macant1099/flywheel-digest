# The DIY Long Tail

## The pattern

Most US membership organizations \u2014 the vast majority by count, not member-weighted \u2014 do not use a perks platform, an AMS deals module, or an affiliate network. They run member deals the way they\u2019ve run them for decades: the club president or volunteer benefits chair negotiates a sponsorship or discount directly with a vendor, and then surfaces it through whatever communication channels the org already owns.

A typical pattern, drawn from car clubs, alumni chapters, hobby groups, and local associations:

1. **Ad-hoc sponsor relationship.** The club signs a sponsorship agreement with a local shop or a national brand. Consideration is usually a flat annual fee or product donation, not a per-transaction commission.
2. **Coupon code in the newsletter.** The monthly email to members includes a code. Sometimes exclusive, often just the vendor\u2019s general public code repackaged.
3. **Forum thread or pinned post.** A members-only forum or Facebook group has a pinned "Member Discounts" thread listing active codes.
4. **Event program insertion.** At a rally, track day, concours, or annual meeting, sponsor offers go in the printed program.
5. **Maybe a static webpage.** A "Member Benefits" page on the club website that is six months out of date.

## Why it persists

Despite being operationally primitive, the DIY pattern survives because it works for the one thing that matters most: **trust and direct endorsement**. A car club president telling 2,000 members "I use this shop and they\u2019re giving us 15% off" outperforms any marketplace discount because members trust the human, not the platform.

The DIY pattern is also zero-cost. A club with a $50/year membership fee and 500 members cannot justify the SaaS fees of a perks platform or an AMS module \u2014 but it can justify one volunteer hour a month to maintain a list.

## Why it breaks

The pattern breaks in five places, every one of them an opportunity for a platform like LiveChannel:

1. **No attribution.** The club cannot demonstrate to a sponsor that the partnership drove sales, which makes renegotiation and growth impossible. Sponsorship stays flat or fades.
2. **Leaked exclusivity.** Coupon codes in a public forum or newsletter reach non-members within days. What was "exclusive" becomes a public discount.
3. **No recurring revenue.** Every sponsor relationship is a one-shot annual negotiation rather than an ongoing revenue stream tied to actual member purchases.
4. **No scale.** The president or benefits chair is the bottleneck. They can sustain maybe five to ten sponsor relationships before the portfolio becomes unmanageable.
5. **No member-identity bridge.** The sponsor has no way to know which members bought, which means no re-marketing, no loyalty mechanics, and no way to improve the offer over time.

## What this tells us about the LiveChannel opportunity

The DIY long tail is the real addressable market for source-side affiliate infrastructure. The large affinity/perks platforms (Abenity, PerkSpot) only serve orgs big enough to justify an enterprise contract. The mainstream affiliate networks (Impact, Rakuten) don\u2019t understand trust-based membership at all. The AMS platforms bolt on shallow "discount directory" features and stop there.

That leaves hundreds of thousands of small-to-mid membership orgs \u2014 car clubs prominent among them \u2014 running on coupon codes and newsletter posts. A platform that preserves their trust-and-endorsement model while adding authentication, attribution, and exclusivity enforcement would be delivering something that doesn\u2019t currently exist at their scale.

## LiveChannel implication

The DIY pattern is not a competitor \u2014 it\u2019s the baseline LiveChannel replaces. The pitch to a club president writes itself: "You\u2019re already doing this. Here\u2019s how you do it with real data, real exclusivity, and real revenue, without any more work than you\u2019re doing today." Every gap in the DIY pattern maps directly to a LiveChannel feature: authenticated-member landing pages, gated links that can\u2019t leak, transaction-level attribution, ongoing revenue share instead of flat sponsorship.

This is the segment to design the car-club deep-dive around.
