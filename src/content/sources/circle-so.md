# Circle

## What the platform is for
Circle is a white-label community SaaS positioned at creators, coaches, B2B operators, and association-style communities that want one branded place for courses, events, memberships, chat, and posts. Hosts pay $89–$419+/month and run their community on a custom domain (and, on Circle Plus, a fully branded mobile app). Commerce inside Circle means selling the host's own offerings — memberships, course bundles, one-time digital products, installment plans — with Circle taking 0.5–4% depending on plan tier, on top of Stripe fees. Circle is widely used but fundamentally a host-sells-to-their-own-members platform, not a multi-vendor commerce network.

## Native deals / affiliate / commerce capability
Circle has two things that sound like "affiliate/deals" but aren't the LiveChannel pattern. First, Circle's own Affiliate Program is a SaaS-referral program ($120 per converted referral, 90-day cookie) — it rewards people who send new hosts to Circle. Second, Circle lets a host configure an in-community affiliate program for their own digital products, with fixed or percentage commissions, so members can earn for referring other members into the host's courses or memberships. Neither surface is a multi-vendor deals marketplace. There is no native way for an outside brand to publish a structured, gated, attributable offer to a Circle community's members. "Perks" and discounts mentioned in Circle's materials are host-controlled incentives (e.g., reward a top member with access to a premium space), not brand-sourced deals.

## What it teaches us about member UX
Circle teaches three things. One, white-label is the expectation in 2026 — members never see "Circle" branding on higher tiers, and the mobile app lives in the app store under the host's name. Two, transaction-fee transparency matters: the published 4% → 0.5% ladder by plan tier is a defensible pricing norm that deals platforms will be measured against. Three, AI agents trained on the community's own content are quickly becoming a default member-facing surface — a deals platform that can't answer "what offers apply to me?" conversationally will feel dated.

## Strengths to learn from
Custom-domain white-label, branded mobile, transparent fee tiers, and a host-configurable affiliate engine are all patterns LiveChannel can emulate in how it presents to sources — especially the idea that a source's members never see the platform brand.

## Gaps as a source-side platform
Circle's affiliate tooling points outbound (the host's product to the host's audience). It has no concept of inbound vendor offers, no offer taxonomy, no exclusivity controls, no multi-brand attribution, and no curated supply side. A source that chose Circle for community still has to solve the members-only-deals layer somewhere else.

## LiveChannel implication
Circle is adjacent, not a competitor: it owns the community and the host's own commerce, and leaves third-party vendor deals completely unaddressed. LiveChannel can plug into Circle-hosted sources as the deals layer their platform doesn't provide, and should match Circle's white-label and fee-transparency norms when selling in. The conceptual lesson — members see the source's brand, never the platform's — is the right default for LiveChannel too.
