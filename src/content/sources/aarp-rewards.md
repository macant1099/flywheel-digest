# AARP Rewards & Member Benefits

## How it works for a member
AARP runs two parallel programs that share a brand. **Member Benefits** is the classic discount catalog — paid AARP members (38M of them, at $20/year) browse aarp.org/benefits or the AARP Now app, find offers from licensed partners across travel, insurance, dining, wireless, eyewear, financial services and more, then redeem by presenting their member number at checkout or using a member-specific code. Dining alone covers 7,000+ restaurant locations with up to 15% off. **AARP Rewards** is a free, gamified loyalty layer: anyone 18+ can join, earn points by taking health quizzes, watching videos, playing games, or syncing a fitness tracker, and redeem points for sweepstakes entries, local deals, or discounted gift cards. Paid AARP members earn points 50% faster (up to 7,500/day vs. 5,000/day) and access a members-only rewards stash. Some benefits also support account linking — e.g., attaching your AARP membership to Exxon Mobil Rewards+ to stack fuel discounts.

## Offer quality and exclusivity
Quality is heterogeneous. On the premium end sit genuinely negotiated deals: AARP-branded UnitedHealthcare Medicare plans (governed by a royalty deal that drew a $9B upfront payment from UnitedHealth in 2024), AARP-rate hotel bookings, dining percent-off at national chains. On the commodity end sit many offers that are effectively public promos wearing an AARP co-brand. The Rewards points layer is explicitly a retention/engagement mechanic, not a savings claim — points expire 12 months after earning, and redemption is in gift cards and sweeps rather than invoice savings. AARP notably does not publish an "average member saves $X/year" figure the way AAA does.

## Operational model
AARP is a nonprofit; AARP Services, Inc. (ASI), a wholly-owned for-profit subsidiary, manages commercial partner relationships. The dominant structure is **brand licensing**: partners pay AARP royalties to use the name and logo and to access the member base. Royalties generated $1.1B of AARP's $1.7B revenue in 2023 — larger than membership dues ($289M). Partnerships are invite-only, deeply vetted, and typically multi-year. The Rewards platform (points, quizzes, games, redemption catalog) is operated in-house by AARP; the benefits themselves are delivered on partner systems.

## What this tells us
Three takeaways. **(1) Brand licensing is the real business.** AARP's $1.1B royalty line is the clearest proof that a trusted source can monetize its member base at scale without running its own merchant acquiring. **(2) A free loyalty/gamification layer is a smart top-of-funnel** — it keeps a 38M-member relationship warm between annual renewals even when a member didn't redeem a benefit that month. **(3) Exclusivity is negotiable if trust is real.** AARP gets away with co-branding public promos because the member assumes vetting has occurred.

## LiveChannel implication
Inspiration — and a more relevant template than AAA, because AARP has essentially productized the idea of "a trusted source monetizing its audience through curated partner deals." LiveChannel's opportunity is to deliver that same economic pattern (licensed/affiliate revenue, dual engagement + discount layers, curated partner catalog) to the 10,000+ sources that will never command AARP's individual brand power but collectively reach comparable audiences. The cautionary note: don't replicate AARP's weakest habit of co-branding public promos — the sources LiveChannel serves are smaller and need truly negotiated exclusivity to justify their place in the value chain.
