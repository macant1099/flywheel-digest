# Abenity

## What they do
Abenity is a white-label perks and discounts platform founded in 2006 by Brian and Mark Roland in Tennessee. The product lets an employer, professional association, or alumni group give its members access to a branded discount marketplace — Abenity supplies the catalog, the portal, and the mobile apps, and the source organization supplies the brand, the member list, and the distribution. Members log in through a source-specific URL (often a vanity domain on the Managed PLUS tier) and browse offers across 100+ retail, travel, and entertainment categories spanning more than one million redemption locations in the US and Canada. Beyond the shared catalog, the platform also supports "private offers" that the source can layer in for its own audience.

From the source's point of view, the workflow is straightforward: subscribe to a plan, plug the SSO API into an existing member system or intranet, and Abenity handles merchant relationships, fulfillment, and content updates. In September 2024 Abenity was acquired by BenefitHub (an Inverness Graham portfolio company), combining Abenity's affinity-organization footprint with BenefitHub's Fortune 1000 employer base.

## Business model
Abenity charges the source organization a SaaS subscription. Published pricing starts at $350/month for the core Perks & Discounts plan (unlimited users, unlimited merchant integrations, dedicated client success) and steps up to $850/month for Managed PLUS, which adds a vanity domain, custom homepage banners, branded emails, and custom offer categories. Members never pay Abenity, and vendors are not charged per-placement slotting fees — merchant economics are handled through Abenity's central catalog relationships.

## Strengths (as a LiveChannel competitor or reference)
- Mature catalog and broad merchant network built up over nearly two decades.
- Genuine white-label posture: the source can put its own brand and domain in front of the program.
- Transparent, self-serve pricing with a real API and SSO flow — unusually open for this category.
- Dual footprint across affinity groups (associations, alumni) and employers.

## Weaknesses / gaps
- The catalog is largely the same discounts every Abenity client exposes — the "private" framing is about access gating, not true source-specific economics.
- UX quality is dated; G2 reviewers specifically call out a clunky interface and app.
- Source organizations get program-level engagement numbers but not deep first-party data about member purchase behavior or intent.
- The product is a destination portal the member navigates to, not a layer embedded inside the source's own community, app, or content surface.

## LiveChannel implication
Direct competitor in the same buyer conversation — when an employer or association is shopping for "a perks program for our members," Abenity is on the shortlist. But Abenity's weaknesses map cleanly onto LiveChannel's thesis: it sells a shared catalog dressed up as private, it returns thin data to the source, and it owns the surface rather than extending the source's own channel. LiveChannel's opportunity is to win the sources that want source-exclusive vendor deals, embedded placement in their own properties, and first-party data back.
